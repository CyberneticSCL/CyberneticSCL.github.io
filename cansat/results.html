The data from our flight demonstration corresponded with the actual flight conditions quite nicely.  We estimated the temperature of the circuitry within the satellite to be at about 75 degrees based on the calibration curve of the thermal sensor.  Note that we placed our thermal sensor such that we were not measuring ambient temperature in the air, but rather the temperature of the circuitry in the satellite. We noticed a slight increase in the rate of the beeps during flight, which corresponded to the chips onboard the CanSat heating up.  Although 75 degrees is still within the operating temperature ranges of the electrical components onboard the spacecraft, overheating is certainly a valid concern when it comes to electronics, and should be taken into consideration when designing more sophisticated spacecraft that are meant to have longer operational lifetimes.

Light level is difficult to quantify.  However, the data we obtained from the light sensor during flight did correspond with the actual light conditions of a cloudy day as well as the calibration points from previous trial runs of the circuit on the ground. 

The payload onboard our CanSat was the demonstration of a deployment mechanism.  Upon ground command, the satellite successfully deployed our dummy payload.
